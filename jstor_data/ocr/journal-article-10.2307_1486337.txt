The Neue Gemeinschaft circle, founded and led by the brothers Hein- rich and Julius Hart, was a period phenomenon related to fin de sidcle aestheticism, Nietzschian heroics, Lebensphilosophie, and the New Roman- ticism.4 Its Weltanschauung was a blend of naive literary utopianism and a breathless, Erlebnis-centered mysticism that had as its aim "the realiza- tion of the highest ideal, the bringing about of the kingdom of fulfillment." The Hart brothers declared that all our loftiest views and aspirations can be translated into fact and life. . . . We can shape our lives according to the loftiest ideas we conceive of, and through learning execute them like a work of art. ... The new Weltanschauung overcomes . . . all oppositions and contradictions which prevailed in the sphere of the old Weltan- schauung-[the tensions between] knowledge [Wissen], will, and life. And with these oppositions, the real motive force of all suffering and strife, all fear and doubt, all despair and misery is overcome as well. This [new Weltanschauung] leads beyond all oppositions to a luminous harmony in the thought, feeling, and life of the individual.5 Philosophically, the justification for this optimism rests upon the Hart brothers' rejection of dualism, i.e., the separation of one's "I" from the objective world. Following this reasoning further leads to the view of the world in which the perceived plurality and tensions of phenomenal reality are immediately resolved; the self-reflecting "I" penetrates its own inner core and therein illumines the inner, harmonious unity of all being. Clearly, social theory written from this quasi-mystical perspective would be bereft of sociological substance. Sociology heuristically accepts the individuated, phenomenal world as ultimate; social life is assumed to be an abiding plurality of sub- jects and institutions in interaction. Moreover, sociology posits that if social life is to be endowed with harmony, it is to be obtained in terms of society's own pluralistic forces, and not by a super-imposition of mystical insight.'4 Specifically, for sociology (as for politics), healing the "crisis" of social life, heuristically requires historical, and not mystical, time. Hence, it is difficult to consider Landauer's (and the Neue Gemeinschaft's) analysis of the crisis of modem Kultur and social life as relevant to the sociological tradition. His speech Alte und neue Gemeinschaft -even if it does adumbrate what is to come-duly reflects their philo- sophical utopianism. Notably, the community to which Buber repeatedly refers is not to be taken-despite an allusion to T6nnies'7-as a socio- logical category. It is rather a kind of Civitas Dei for those initiated in Erlebnis mysticism. Indeed, this "neue Gemeinschaft" cannot be identified with any specific group of individuals living in a definite framework of social relations, because, as Buber asserts: Many belong to the Neue Gemeinschaft whom we have never seen. Even if they have different visions and thoughts from ours, the bond uniting us is far greater and deeper than that which we have with men with whom we may share opinions on this or that issue.'8 It is of interest that despite the thorough transformation of Buber's thought in ensuing years, this type of thinking, with its tendency to spirit- ualize the experience of community as well as to appreciate socio-political issues in terms of man's inner life, recurs with a fair degree of regularity. More than half a century later, in an essay entitled "The Validity and Limitations of Political Principle," Buber returns to this notion of a great spiritual Gemeinschaft that transcends sociological specificity. There is, it seems to me, a front-only seldom perceived by those who compose it-that cuts across all the fronts of the hour, both the internal and the external. There they stand ranged side by side, the men of real conviction who are found in all groups, all parties, all peoples, yet who know little or nothing of one another from party to party, from people to people. As different as the goals are in one place and in another, it is still one front, for they are all engaged in one fight for human truth .... The goals are different, very different, but if each way has been trod in truth, the lines leading to these goals intersect, extend beyond them, in the truth of God. Those who stand at the crossfront, those who know nothing of one another, have to do with one another.'9 Moreover, the idea that there exists a universal spiritual community that is grounded in "the innermost core of our hidden being" (Landauer's words), mutatis mutandis, is at the heart of much that is central to the philosophy of I and Thou.
